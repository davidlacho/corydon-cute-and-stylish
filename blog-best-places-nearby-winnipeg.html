The Best Places Nearby in Winnipeg

📍 Winnipeg Guides • ⏱️ 16 min read • 📅 April 13, 2026

This is my working Winnipeg guidebook—the list I refine after years hosting on Airbnb since 2011—for guests who stay at Corydon Cute & Stylish in Crescentwood. It mixes walkable Corydon favourites with short drives and a few splurge-worthy destinations across the city. Treat it as a starting list—hours, menus, and reservations change, so always confirm before you go.

How to use this guide: Spots are grouped by food and drink, sightseeing, neighbourhoods, groceries, leisure, and a little local vocabulary. Each photo matches our live Airbnb guidebook so you can recognize storefronts before you go. For deeper dives, see our guides to Corydon Village, The Forks, the Canadian Museum for Human Rights, and the Exchange District.

Food scene

From everyday coffee to special-occasion dining, these are the names that come up again and again when people ask what to eat near the house.

Cheese, bread, cafés, and everyday bites

  • The Cheesemongers Fromagerie — A great cheese shop; they also carry bread from Gato Bakery on Saturdays.
  • Gâto Bakeshop — Excellent sourdough and treats.
  • Forgotten Flavours — Maria and Chris Holbrow started baking in Niverville, MB; demand grew from farmers' markets into a larger facility, and they took over a Corydon Avenue lease in late 2024. The former Pennyloaf Bakery space has serious bread on the shelves again.
  • Thom Bargen Coffee Roasters — Great coffee and a natural wine bar at one location; the original Thom Bargen is a perfect excuse to stroll Wolseley.
  • MAKE Coffee + Stuff — Spacious, laptop-friendly, and easy for a focused morning.
  • Café Carlo — A reliable nearby favourite.
  • French Way Café — French café energy—baguettes, lunch, and a proper sit-down.
  • Eva's Gelato & Coffee Bar — Nearby gelato when you want something cold and sweet.
  • Clementine Café — In my book, the best brunch in Winnipeg. Arrive hungry and expect a wait on weekends.

Little Italy and Corydon classics

  • Bar Italia — A Little Italy staple.
  • Langside Grocery — Strong cocktails in an intimate setting.
  • The Roost on Corydon — Great cocktails and finger food.
  • Falafel Place — Huge portions and fair prices—ideal after a long day of sightseeing.

Restaurants worth planning around

  • Passero — One of Winnipeg's best restaurants; worth dressing up and booking ahead.
  • Deer + Almond — A flagship for the city's creative dining scene.
  • Harth Mozza & Wine Bar — A bit farther from the Airbnb, but absolutely worth the trip.
  • Peasant Cookery — Downtown favourite where classic and modern French ideas meet Manitoba comfort.
  • Oval Room Brasserie — Restored to its 1913 look; Canadian brasserie cooking with a serious cocktail and wine program (breakfast through dinner).
  • Feast Cafe Bistro — Indigenous-owned bistro with warm hospitality.
  • Petit Socco — Seasonal three-course set menu, reservations required, two seatings. Open Wednesday–Friday for dinner from 6:00 p.m.
  • Enoteca — Now rebranded as Né de Loup; Chef Scott Bagshaw remains one of the city's culinary leaders.
  • Buvette — Wine-bar and small-plates energy when you want something refined without a full tasting menu.

Fish, chips, deli, and pub fare

  • The Grove Pub & Restaurant — Delicious fish and chips.
  • Luda's Deli — Real Ukrainian-Manitoban food; I grew up eating here. The surrounding area is rougher than Crescentwood, but the food is worth the drive.

Beer, wine, and after-dark

  • The Beer Can — A fun summer spot for local brews.
  • Low Life Barrel House — Great beer and natural wines.
  • Good Neighbour Brewing Company — Craft brewery within walking distance of the house.
  • Sookram's Brewing Company — Another strong craft option nearby.
  • Nonsuch Brewing Co. — Belgian-inspired beers and a polished taproom experience.
  • Patent 5 Distillery — Local spirits when you want something beyond beer.
  • Ellement Wine + Spirits — My pick for the best wine shop in the city.
  • Grant Park Liquor Mart — Closest large-format liquor store to the house with a strong selection.
  • Amsterdam Tea Room and Bar — Fully licensed European-style tea room in the Exchange—ideal for a celebration that is not steakhouse-loud.
  • Cargo Bar — Winnipeg's shipping-container mobile bar: a modern patio pop-up when it is in season.

Pizza

  • Shorty's Pizza — My favourite pizza in Winnipeg.

Host tip: If you are deciding between a reservation spot (Passero, Deer + Almond, Petit Socco) and a walk-in Corydon night, book the special meal first, then build the rest of your trip around it.

Sightseeing

Mix iconic Winnipeg landmarks with low-key neighbourhood stops.

  • Enderton Park (Peanut Park) — A beautiful pocket park on the street; we have a full Peanut Park guide for guests.
  • The Leaf — Do not miss the butterfly garden; pair it with Assiniboine Park for a full afternoon.
  • Canadian Museum for Human Rights — Plan several hours; the architecture and exhibits reward slow pacing.
  • The Forks — National Historic Site energy—markets, river trails, and six thousand years of human stories in one meeting place.
  • True North Square — Food, shops, and Jets energy when the team is home.
  • Thermea Spa Village Winnipeg — Nordic spa rituals in a calm, beautifully designed setting.
  • Assiniboine Park — Zoo, gardens, walking paths, and room to breathe.

Neighbourhoods

  • Little Italy — Gelato, espresso, red-sauce classics, and Corydon's main strip.
  • Wolseley — Grand old homes, small markets, and excellent food woven into a walkable grid.
  • Wellington Crescent — Leafy walking paths past some of Winnipeg's most stately homes.
  • Exchange District — Warehouses turned galleries, cafés, and design shops—ideal for a camera walk.
  • Crescentwood — Our neighbourhood—quiet residential streets, mature trees, and quick access to Corydon.

Grocery

  • Foodfare Stores — Close by, with a particularly good butcher counter.
  • Safeway (River Avenue) — A straightforward mid-size run when you need everything in one cart.
  • Red River Co-op Food Store — Another solid Co-op option when you are already in that part of town.

Leisure

  • Plain Bicycle – Bicycle Garden — Non-profit bike rentals, including winter setups when you want to try cycling in the snow.
  • Qaumajuq — Innovative museum home to the world's largest public collection of contemporary Inuit art—see also our WAG & Qaumajuq visitor guide.

City advice: customs, culture, and phrases

Indigenous Peoples of Manitoba

Canada recognizes three groups of Indigenous Peoples: First Nations, Inuit, and Métis. Manitoba sits on the traditional territories of Cree, Dakota, Dene, Ojibway, and Oji-Cree First Nations, as well as the homeland of the Métis Nation. Just over half of Indigenous people in Canada live in urban centres—including Winnipeg—so respectful curiosity and support for Indigenous-led businesses (like Feast) matters. For more context, read our Indigenous Winnipeg guide for visitors.

Speak like a Manitoban

We borrow vocabulary from diners, church basements, and long winters. A few favourites:

  • Fat boy — A house-made beef patty topped with chili—Winnipeg's answer to the loose-meat obsession.
  • Honey dill — Mayo, honey, and dill whisked into the dip you did not know you needed with chicken fingers.
  • Dainties — Bite-sized home baking served by the platter at gatherings.
  • A social — A fundraiser—often for a wedding, sports team, or community hall—built around tickets, raffles, and dainties.
  • Booter — Spring misery when meltwater fills your boot after you misjudge a puddle.

Respectful travel: Some entries (for example Luda's Deli) sit in grittier parts of the city. Use the same street smarts you would in any unfamiliar neighbourhood—lock the car, hide bags, and visit during daylight if you are unsure.
